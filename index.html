History & Heritage

Maheshwari weaving dates to the 5th century Holkar dynasty. Banarasi brocade was first mentioned in the Rig Veda. Phulkari embroidery began as dowry preparation in rural Punjab. These aren't just fabrics — they're living archives.

the making of

Silk Reeling

Raw silk cocoons are carefully boiled and the delicate filaments drawn out by hand, then spun into fine threads on a traditional charkha. This is where every Chokhi saree begins.

Natural Dyeing

Threads are immersed in natural dye baths — indigo, madder, and turmeric — absorbing colour gradually. Each shade deepens over time, never rushed.

Hand Weaving

On traditional pit looms, every thread is interlaced by hand. A single saree takes 15–45 days to complete, shaped by rhythm, precision, and years of practice.

Hand Weaving · 24 years

Rekha Bai

Maheshwar, Madhya Pradesh

Rekha has been weaving on a traditional pit loom since she was fourteen, taught by her mother in the courtyard of their home beside the Narmada. Each piece she creates carries the rhythm of that river.

She specialises in the distinctive Maheshwari reversible border — a technique that requires complete focus and can only be done in the early morning light.

See Her Work →

A day at the loom

Rekha wakes before sunrise. By the time the light touches the Narmada, she is already at her loom — the same pit loom her mother used, repaired dozens of times but never replaced. The sound of the shuttle moving through the warp threads is the first sound her children hear every morning.

A single Maheshwari saree takes between five and seven days to complete. The counting of threads — sometimes over 3,000 per saree — happens entirely from memory. "My hands remember what my eyes cannot see," she says.

Rekha is also one of the last weavers in Maheshwar who still weaves the traditional nine-yard version of the saree, a form that is increasingly rare as fashion shifts toward the six-yard drape.

01

Warping

Threading the loom with individual silk strands — up to 3,200 threads per saree — stretched precisely to maintain an even tension throughout the weave.

02

Pattern Setting

Tying the jacquard or dobby cards that will guide the pattern. Rekha's reversible borders require a separate set of instructions for each side.

03

Weaving

The shuttle passes back and forth, interlacing the weft threads with the warp. At full pace, Rekha completes roughly four inches of cloth per hour.

Our Story

Born from the banks
of the Narmada

We believe the most beautiful things take time

Chokhi began with a simple question: why are the most extraordinary textiles in the world so hard to find? The weavers of Maheshwar, Chanderi, and Varanasi have been creating masterworks for centuries — yet their craft was disappearing, overshadowed by fast fashion and industrialised imitation.

We set out to change that. Not with charity, but with commerce. By connecting these master weavers directly with people who genuinely value what handcraft means — time, patience, mastery, and the irreducible warmth of human hands — we create a market that rewards the slow way of making things.

Every piece on Chokhi is made to order. Nothing sits in a warehouse. Nothing is made before it is asked for. That's not a constraint — it's the point.

01

Made to Order

Nothing is made until it is asked for. Every saree begins its journey the day you place your order — ensuring zero waste and complete intention.

02

Fair at the Source

Our weavers receive a minimum of 60% of the sale price, directly deposited within 7 days of dispatch. No middlemen. No negotiation.

03

Craft over Speed

A Maheshwari saree takes 5–7 days to weave. We are proud of that. Delivery times reflect the reality of making something well.
